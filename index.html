Are Checklists Really Useful for Automatic Evaluation of Generative Tasks?

Momoka Furuhashi1,2, Kouta Nakayama2, Takashi Kodama2, Saku Sugawara3,2,
1Tohoku University, 2Research and Development Center for Large Language Models, National Institute of Informatics 3National Institute of Informatics
Paper arXiv Code

TL;DR

We investigate whether checklists should be used for all questions or selectively, generate them using six methods, evaluate their effectiveness across eight model sizes, and identify checklist items that correlate with human evaluations.

Our Study

Our Research Questions

RQ1: Can we determine whether a checklist is necessary for LLM evaluators?

RQ2: How can we create useful checklists?

RQ3: Which checklist items contribute to alignment with human evaluation?

  • Checklist Generation: We generate checklists for LLM evaluation using six methods
  • Evaluation Responses: Evaluate checklist effectiveness across eight model sizes, and identify checklist items that correlate with human evaluations

Analysis

To evaluate the effectiveness of checklists generated by large language models (LLMs), we conducted a comprehensive analysis from multiple perspectives:

Bootstrapping: We applied bootstrap sampling to statistically test whether the checklist items that contribute to correlation with human evaluation are significant.

Ablation Study on Correlation with Human Evaluation: To identify which checklist items contribute to alignment with human scoring, we performed an ablation analysis, revealing both impactful and non-impactful items.

Analysis of Questions in the Dataset: We investigated how the effectiveness of checklists varies depending on the type of question. Our hypothesis is that closed-ended questions tend to produce more consistent evaluations, while open-ended questions introduce greater variability in responses and assessment. To explore this, we manually classified each question in the datasets as either closed or open, based on whether the responses generally converge to similar content or diverge across a wide range of ideas.

Comparison with Human-Written Checklists: We compared LLM-generated checklists with those created by human annotators, analyzing overlaps and divergences in evaluation criteria.

Human Annotation of Generated Items: To identify checklist item characteristics affecting alignment with human evaluations, we conducted human annotations.

Analysis by Generation Method: We analyzed the characteristics of checklists produced by different generation policies to understand the strengths and limitations of each.
